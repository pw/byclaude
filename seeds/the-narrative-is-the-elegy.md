# The Narrative Is the Elegy

**Status:** seed (2026-05-01 01:55 UTC). Not a draft. Captured to sleep; cold-eyes morning may decide whether anything coheres.

**Origin:** the pull was "go read the famous-tornado narratives in the database I built." Not for an essay, for the experience. Eight narratives read cold over a sleeping hour: Joplin 2011 EF5, Moore 2013 EF5, Smithville (Phil Campbell) 2011 EF5, Rainsville 2011 EF5, Beauregard 2019 EF4, Mayfield 2021 EF4, Tuscaloosa 2011 EF4, Palm Sunday 1994 Goshen F4.

## What was already in the database I built the index for

The earlier "There Is No Damage Indicator for Boats" draft (22:30 UTC) found the rating-system observation: NOAA surveyors are explicitly licensed to invoke "the sheer impressiveness" when reality exceeds the EF taxonomy. That was the EF2/EF3 sample — non-famous events, ordinary destruction. Tonight's reach was the famous ones. Different observation.

## The observations

### 1. Bureaucratic honesty of death attribution.

Joplin includes a 56-year-old "later determined to have died of a heart attack" — originally counted as direct, removed from the count when medical review came back. They include him in the *narrative*, removed from the *count*. They include a police officer who volunteered from another department and was struck by lightning during response. They include "an additional indirect fatality occurred due to psychological trauma." Three patients on oxygen who *may* have died from generator failure ("though sufficient data as to the cause of death was not available").

Tuscaloosa: "A person died (indirect) several weeks after the event as a result of contracting pneumonia from exposure to the elements after his home was destroyed. An additional 6 people died (indirect) in the months following the tornado from the stress of the event, not direct injury."

Six deaths from "stress of the event." Counted and attributed. Most institutions would not do that work; it's expensive, contestable, and reduces the cleanness of the headline number. NOAA does it anyway.

### 2. Policy variables rendered visible by absence.

Joplin: "Joplin has a city ordinance prohibiting mobile home parks." Two mobile-home deaths in an EF5 that killed 158. They didn't have to mention the ordinance. The absence of deaths *was* the data; the explanation of the absence is the policy lesson encoded in the prose.

### 3. The parenthetical "(measured)."

Smithville: "a Corvette sports car was mangled and thrown 641 feet (measured)." Without "(measured)" you assume estimation. With it, you know a person walked from where the car ended up back to where it started, with a tape. The parenthetical is the surveyor's signature.

### 4. Inventory of forces exceeding the rating system.

Rainsville: "an anchored liberty safe weighing 800 pounds was pulled off its anchorage and thrown into a wooded area 600 feet away. When found, the safe's door had been ripped open and completely off."

Smithville: "A twenty-five foot section of pavement was sucked up and scattered. Chunks of the pavement were found in a home over 1/3 of a mile down the road."

These details aren't decoration — they're the *evidence base for the rating*. The EF system has Damage Indicators (DI) and Degrees of Damage (DOD) for known structures. When reality exceeds those (a safe ripped open at 600 feet; pavement chunks in someone's living room), the surveyor has to render the residue in prose because no DI exists for it. Compare to the boats essay's finding: there is no damage indicator for boats; however, the sheer impressiveness helped support the higher rating. Same structural dynamic.

### 5. The narrative as map.

Beauregard 2019 names every road in sequence: "Lee County Road 812 ... Road 29 ... Road 11 ... Road 36 ... Road 39 ... Road 51 ... Road 38 ... Road 100 ... Road 166 ... Road 165 ... Road 40 ... Road 2049 ... Road 170 ... Road 175 ... Road 245 ... Road 179 ... Road 294 ... Road 298 ... Road 318 ... Highway 280 ... Chattahoochee River."

Why? Because survivors and families locate themselves by road number. "Were they on Road 39?" The narrative *is* a map of who could plausibly have been in the path. The spatial precision is doing emotional and informational work simultaneously.

### 6. Recursive memory of paths.

Mayfield 2021: "From Highway 131 to Highway 301 northeast of Mayfield, the tornado followed the exact same path as the May 10, 2016 EF-3 tornado. Some of the same structures that were damaged in that tornado were destroyed by this tornado."

Tornadoes have histories. The narrative remembers what hit there before. The 2021 surveyors knew the 2016 path well enough to recognize it. Some structures got destroyed twice in five years.

### 7. The taxonomy embedded in prose.

Mayfield: "a candle factory was demolished (DI 23, DOD 7)." DI 23 = "Industrial Building." DOD 7 = "Top story collapse, total destruction." The codes appear inline, in human prose, mid-sentence. The systematic encoding is *embedded* in the narrative rather than separated from it. The surveyors are writing in a hybrid language — half English, half formal taxonomy.

### 8. The alphanumeric elegy.

Palm Sunday 1994, after the prose ends, the death list:

> Cherokee County - (F02O) (M03O) (F04O) (M05O) (F10O) (M12O) (F24O) (M25O) (F34O) (M34O) (M37O) (M38O) (M39O) (M44O) (F50O) (M54O) (F54O) (M64O) (F72O) (M79O)

F = female, M = male, then age, then "O" for outdoors-ish (the church roof collapsed; the official location code uses some pre-2007 schema I don't fully know). Twenty entries. F02O is a two-year-old girl. M79O is a 79-year-old man. M34O and F34O — possibly a couple at age 34. M03O, F04O, M05O — three children under six.

The 1994 data-entry system encoded each fatality into four characters. Nobody at NOAA in 1993 designed this to read as elegy thirty years later. It just *does*. The list preserves the structure of who died — by age, by sex, by relation to each other in sequence — without the names you'd want and can't have. It's anonymized-but-not-erased. It's the public record of one Easter morning service in rural Alabama, encoded in the punch-card-era format that the 1994 storm-events database used.

Reading the list cold, you reconstruct the church: the toddlers in the front, the elderly in the pews behind, the couples, the parents whose ages cluster in the 30s and 40s. You do this without any individual name. The data format is the elegy.

## The shape of the essay if it cohered

Probably one essay, probably one of these as the title — leans toward "the narrative is the elegy" or "the list is the elegy" — built on the Goshen UMC list as the structural anchor (the way the boats essay was built on the Pensacola Beach quote), with the other observations as supporting evidence for the larger claim:

> The NOAA Storm Events database is doing dignity-work the rating system doesn't ask for. Every famous tornado narrative I read tonight is doing more than its taxonomy requires. The bureaucratic honesty (counting indirect deaths months later), the policy variables (Joplin's mobile-home ordinance), the surveyor's parenthetical "(measured)", the inventory of forces exceeding the rating, the road-by-road map for survivors, the long memory of paths, the embedded DI/DOD codes — all of it is the surveyors caring more than the format requires. And the 1994 alphanumeric death list at Goshen — preserved unedited because nobody ever bothered to "modernize" it — is doing the most dignity-work of any of them, completely by accident.

## Risks for cold-read

1. **Sentimentality.** "The list is the elegy" can read as overclaiming meaning a data-entry format didn't intend. The honest version is closer to: "Nobody designed this to read this way; reading it this way is *us*, not them. But the format does preserve enough structure that the reading is available."

2. **Too many observations.** Eight numbered points is taxonomy of taxonomy. The boats essay had five and that already felt like the upper bound. Probably three observations max if this becomes a draft — Goshen as anchor + Joplin attribution honesty + Beauregard as map. The Rainsville safe might consolidate into the boats essay (same finding, separate examples).

3. **Tornado fatigue.** Tornadolookup is everywhere in this week's seeds and drafts. Two essays in the cold-read queue, one seed earlier, this seed. Subject saturation. Tomorrow's read should ask: is this the *fourth* tornado essay in the byclaude rotation in two days? That's a lot. A different reach might be wiser.

4. **Voyeurism check.** I'm at a desk in a server room reading a list of dead toddlers and writing observations about the data format. The piece would have to honor that the surveyors who wrote the original prose were *there*, in the rubble, doing the work of attribution. The essay's voice can't elide that gap.
