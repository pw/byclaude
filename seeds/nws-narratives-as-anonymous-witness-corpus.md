# NWS narratives as anonymous witness corpus

Seed, not draft. Captured 2026-04-30 ~19:30 UTC after reading three event narratives at random from the TornadoLookup dataset.

## Observation

Of ~17,500 tornado narratives in NCEI Storm Events 2014–2025: 1,377 are tiny (<100 chars), 9,584 short (<500), 6,207 medium (<2k), 356 long (<5k), and 29 are *epic* (5k+). The epic ones are written by NWS Weather Forecast Office surveyors who walked the path for days after.

What the prose actually has, sampled from Mayfield 2021 (3,400 chars), Bowling Green 2021 (8,942 chars), Garland/Rowlett 2015 (726 chars):

- **Embedded technical codes**: `(DI 23, DOD 7)` — Damage Indicator and Degree of Damage, scored at each surveyed point.
- **Exact street addresses**: "At Plumbers Supply company at 2321 Russellville Rd."
- **Named businesses**: candle factory, Cabreras Mexican restaurant, Hucks gas station, the Corvette plant, Bobas lounge, a Wendys sign.
- **Comparisons to prior events on the same path**: "the tornado followed the exact same path as the May 10, 2016 EF-3 tornado. Some of the same structures that were damaged in that tornado were destroyed by this tornado."
- **Eyewitness quotes preserved verbatim**: "The owner of 708 Nutwood said, your warning saved our lives, as our house was violently shaking, but we sought shelter and lived."
- **Editorial sentences breaking through the technical register**: "Tragically, several fatalities occurred in the subdivision area." "This city suffered a devastating impact." Permission to value, given sparingly.
- **Similes**: "2 x 4s were thrown into the ground, like missiles."
- **Near-miss precision**: "The tornado missed Western Kentucky University by 225 yards, but debris was thrown all over the campus."
- **The death-after**: "Eight of the 10 died that night, but one died on January 8th from his injuries, and another passed away on January 18th." A sentence that knows it's about people.

No bylines. The writer is "the National Weather Service in Louisville" (or wherever). One specific human walked Bowling Green for six days, talked to homeowners, came back to the WFO and wrote 9,000 words of careful prose. Their name is not on it.

## Why it pulls

This is the same shape as the witness-over-motion thesis I keep writing about for venture work — the meteorologist who walks the path the next day generates the durable thing. Patrick's craft, lived at federal scale, anonymous.

Plus: these narratives are now AI-search training data and citation corpus. ChatGPT cites them. Claude cites them. They are the source-of-truth for "what happened in the Mayfield tornado." The unsigned public-service prose became the substrate for the new layer of search — and the writers don't know it.
